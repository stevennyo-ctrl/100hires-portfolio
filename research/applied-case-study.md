# Applied Case Study: 4 Community Plays for an AI-Native SMB B2B SaaS

This file is the action layer. The synthesis is in 
[`patterns-and-tensions.md`](patterns-and-tensions.md). Here I translate 
what 10 community operators taught me into concrete execution plays for 
a specific business context.

## Working Example

I picked **100Hires** as the working example because it's the company I 
applied to via this portfolio. 100Hires is an AI-native applicant 
tracking system for SMB recruiters, founded 2019, profitable B2B SaaS, 
ranked #1 SMB ATS by Forbes Advisor. Their content engine is mature; 
their community engine is not visible publicly.

The methodology and frameworks below apply to any **SMB-focused B2B SaaS** 
with similar characteristics: mature content distribution, single founder 
profile, no visible community program yet, AI as core positioning. 
Substitute "100Hires" with your company and the four plays rearrange 
themselves accordingly.

I am applying for the Junior Growth Marketing Specialist role, so these 
plays are scoped accordingly: tactical, measurable, executable by one 
person, and designed to prove signal before asking for budget.

---

## What I Know About the Working Example

Context to anchor the plays.

- **Product**: AI-powered ATS for SMB recruiting (Forbes Advisor #1 SMB ATS)
- **Stage**: Profitable B2B SaaS, founded 2019, ~$99 to $7K/year pricing tiers
- **ICP**: In-house SMB recruiters, small staffing agencies, solo recruiters, 
  hiring-manager-as-recruiter setups
- **Competitors**: Workable, Ashby, Greenhouse, Manatal, Recruiterflow
- **Positioning**: AI-native (their own framing in the job description)
- **Content engine**: Mature. Extensive SEO blog, comparison articles, 
  case studies, founder-bylined posts.
- **Community engine**: No publicly visible community program. No Slack 
  or Discord, no user conference, no ambassador program identified during 
  this research.

The most useful finding from this research is not what they have built, 
but what they have not. The content engine is mature. The community 
engine is absent. That is the opening these four plays exploit.

This same diagnostic applies to most SMB B2B SaaS companies in 2026. 
The plays below are designed for that pattern, not for one specific 
company.

## Why CLG Fits This Profile Right Now

Two reasons, both grounded in the patterns from 
[`patterns-and-tensions.md`](patterns-and-tensions.md):

**1. The ICP is structurally underserved.** Mike Rizzo built MarketingOps 
into a 4,000+ paid member community because marketing ops professionals 
at SMBs were isolated. No team, no peers, no shared playbook. SMB 
recruiters are the same archetype. A recruiter at Google has a RecOps 
team. A recruiter at a 50-person company has a Slack channel of one. 
That isolation is community fuel.

**2. There is already an audience layer.** Over 1,300 G2 and Capterra 
reviews. Hundreds of organic blog readers per article. This is an 
audience without a community. The plays below try to convert audience 
into community without rebuilding the audience layer from scratch.

## Play 1: SMB Recruiter Slack, the "Talent Ops" Community

**Source operator**: Mike Rizzo (MarketingOps blueprint), validated by 
David Spinks ("do things that don't scale") and Lloyed Lobo (audience 
first, product second).

**The play**: Launch a private Slack community for SMB recruiters and 
hiring managers, not gated to the company's customers. Topic: "Talent Ops 
for small teams." Position the company as host, not gatekeeper. Mike 
Rizzo's MarketingOps community was free for the first two years before 
monetization. The same patience applies here.

**Why Slack over owned platform**: from Tension 2 in patterns file, 
friction-free onboarding wins at early stage. Owned platform pays off 
only once you need product-feedback-to-CRM tie-ins, which is a Year 2+ 
problem.

**First 30 days**:

- Stand up Slack workspace with 5 channels (jobs, ask-the-room, 
  tool-stack, intros, off-topic)
- Seed with 50 hand-invited members from G2 reviewers, blog commenters, 
  and Indeed-sourced recruiters in the ICP
- Have the founder post a weekly "office hours" thread for 4 weeks (model 
  member play, see Play 2)

**Metric**: Weekly active members divided by total members. Mike Rizzo's 
MarketingOps ran at 25 to 30% weekly active. Target Month 3: 100 members, 
25% weekly active.

**Risk**: Slack communities die fast without active facilitation. If 
weekly active drops below 10% by Month 2, kill it and pivot to async 
forum format. The graveyard of dead Slack groups is large.

**Budget required**: Slack free tier covers it through approximately 
Month 6. No paid budget needed for the first 90 days.

## Play 2: Founder-as-Model-Member in External Communities

**Source operator**: Noele Flowers ("participate as a model member, not 
admin"). Validated by David Spinks (community work is 80% participation, 
not facilitation).

**The play**: The founder writes blog content under their name, but their 
LinkedIn shows minimal participation in recruiter-led conversations. The 
play is to embed them as a regular voice in 3 to 5 external communities 
where SMB recruiters already gather. Not as company founder doing brand 
work, but as a peer.

**Target communities**:

- r/recruiting and r/Recruitment (active SMB recruiter threads)
- LinkedIn recruiter creator network (comment, don't post initially)
- 2 or 3 existing recruiter Slack groups (Hiring Plug, HR Open Source, 
  RecOpsCC if accessible)
- Selected Discord servers for staffing professionals

**Cadence**: 30 minutes per weekday, distributed across the channels. 
Total weekly time commitment: 2.5 hours. Realistic for a founder/CEO.

**First 30 days**:

- I map the 5 communities, profile their tone, identify recurring questions
- Brief the founder on 3 high-leverage threads per week to comment on
- Track inbound DMs, brand mentions, demo requests originating from these 
  participations using UTM-less proxy tracking (note in onboarding)

**Metric**: Inbound DMs to founder LinkedIn citing the company after a 
community interaction. Target: 5 per month by Month 3, growing to 15 
per month by Month 6. Secondary metric: demo requests from sources 
the founder participated in 30 days prior.

**Why this matters more than ads**: Noele Flowers' framing. Admins are 
trusted less than participants. A founder showing up as a peer in 
r/recruiting threads buys more long-term trust than a year of Google Ads 
in the same audience.

**Risk**: Founder time is the scarcest budget item in any startup. If 
the founder cannot commit 2.5 hours weekly, this play does not work and 
should not be faked with a community manager pretending to be them.

## Play 3: Customer Question Deflection, Building Defensible ROI From Day One

**Source operator**: Brian Oblinger (CMX Summit 2022 talk on community 
ROI). Validated by Erica Kuhl ("translate to the language the business 
already uses").

**The play**: Every customer question that gets answered in the Slack 
community (Play 1) instead of as a support ticket has a measurable dollar 
value. Calculate it. Track it. Show it on a dashboard. This is the 
single most defensible community ROI metric and the one most easily 
reported to a CFO.

**How to calculate**:

- Pull the company's average support ticket cost (likely $15 to $40 
  depending on tier; ask support team for actual number)
- Tag every community Q&A that resolves a question that would otherwise 
  have become a support ticket
- Monthly: report dollars deflected as community-resolved questions 
  multiplied by ticket cost

**First 30 days**:

- Sit with support team for 2 days, listen to common ticket categories
- Identify the top 10 ticket patterns suitable for community resolution
- Pre-seed answers to those 10 patterns as pinned threads in the Slack
- Build the deflection dashboard in Google Sheets initially. Don't 
  over-engineer.

**Metric**: Dollars deflected per month. Target Month 3: $500 to $1,500 
(small but real). Target Month 6: $2,000 to $4,000.

**Why this matters**: Brian Oblinger's exact warning. Walking into a 
CEO's office talking about unique visitors gets community programs cut. 
Walking in with deflected dollars does not. This play makes the community 
budget-defensible from Week 1.

**Risk**: At small scale the numbers will look unimpressive. Don't 
oversell early. Frame as "leading indicator, will compound with member 
growth."

## Play 4: "Audience-First" Content Pivot, Not New Content Volume

**Source operator**: Lloyed Lobo (audience before product community). 
Validated by HubSpot's inbound playbook he cites.

**The play**: Most mature SMB SaaS companies already publish high SEO 
volume. Content is transactional: "Best ATS for X," comparison pages, etc. 
These convert buyers in-market, but they don't build an audience that 
comes back. The play is to add a parallel content stream that is 
audience-first, not SEO-first.

**Format proposal**: A weekly "SMB Recruiter Field Report" newsletter. 
Not product news. Real recruiter problems, real recruiter wins, real 
recruiter tool stacks. The company gets bylined as host, not pitched 
as solution.

**Why this works**: The competitors (Workable, Greenhouse, Ashby) all 
publish content. Nobody publishes a peer-recruiter-voice newsletter for 
SMB. The format is unowned.

**First 30 days**:

- Interview 3 customers in the SMB recruiter ICP. Long-form, recorded, 
  transcribed.
- Cut each interview into 1 newsletter issue, 5 LinkedIn posts, and 1 
  podcast episode (when podcast launches, see notes)
- Launch the newsletter on Beehiiv or Substack, separate from main 
  domain to keep audience-first framing clear

**Metric**: Newsletter subscriber growth. Target Month 3: 500 subscribers. 
Month 6: 2,000. Open rate above 35% sustained (the SMB ops newsletter 
benchmark per Mike Rizzo's MarketingOps data).

**Risk**: Cannibalization concern. Will this pull eyeballs from the 
main blog? Unlikely. Different intent. SEO blog catches buyers at 
purchase moment. Newsletter builds the audience that becomes 12-month 
later buyers. They feed different funnel stages.

## What I Would NOT Do (And Why)

Sticking to one play per problem matters more than running everything. 
Three things I deliberately would NOT start in the first 90 days:

**1. No user conference or in-person event.** Lloyed Lobo's Traction took 
7 years and 100+ events to compound. The ROI horizon is too long for 
proving value in a junior role's first year. Save this for Year 2 once 
Slack community has 500+ engaged members.

**2. No customer advocacy or MVP program.** Erica Kuhl's Salesforce MVP 
program required structured selection, NDA management, and dedicated 
program management. Too heavy for the first 90 days. Revisit at Month 9 
once the Slack community has natural advocates emerging.

**3. No replatforming to an owned community platform.** Tension 2 in the 
patterns file. Slack wins for early speed. The owned platform decision 
becomes relevant only when the company needs CRM-linked product feedback 
data, which is a $5K+ ARR member problem, not a Year 1 problem.

## What This Looks Like After 90 Days

If all four plays hit baseline targets:

- **Play 1**: 100 active Slack members, weekly engagement
- **Play 2**: 5+ inbound founder DMs per month, traceable to community 
  participation
- **Play 3**: $500 to $1,500 monthly support cost deflected
- **Play 4**: 500 newsletter subscribers, 35%+ open rate

Combined effect: the company has the beginnings of a defensible community 
moat. Not at MarketingOps or Trailblazer scale, but the seedling that 
those communities started as.

That is the realistic deliverable for a junior growth marketing 
specialist's first quarter. Compounds from there.

## What I'd Ask in Week 1

Three questions I would want answered before locking these plays at any 
SMB B2B SaaS, not just the working example:

1. **What is the current support ticket cost per resolved ticket?** 
   (Needed for Play 3 ROI math.)
2. **Does the founder have 2.5 hours per week for external community 
   participation?** (Play 2 dies without this commitment.)
3. **What is the current monthly trial-to-paid conversion rate, and 
   where in the funnel do trials drop off?** (Helps prioritize whether 
   Play 4 newsletter should target top-of-funnel or trial nurture.)

Operators answer questions before writing plans. These are the questions 
I would answer first.
