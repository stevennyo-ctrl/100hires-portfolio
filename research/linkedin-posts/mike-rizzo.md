## Post 2 — Data discipline before AI deployment (signal from the MO Pros community)

**Date**: 2026-06-14 (approx, "1week")
**Link**: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mikedrizzo_marketingops-ai-gtm-activity-7469720360582725632-47je?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFZQPK8BcXnoBwVbAgFxMUiN2Lddij6pz3k
**Engagement**: 42 likes, 5 comments

52% of organizations offer no formal training for their MOps teams (2025 State of the MO Pro research)

While those same teams are being asked to implement AI, maintain existing systems, execute campaigns, and show ROI on all of it.

And somehow, the budget for AI tools is never the problem.

The MOps teams I watch getting real results with AI share one thing.

They spent 2022 and 2023 boring everyone in the room with data governance.

They documented processes before automating them.

They defined what a "lead" meant before building a scoring model.

And they cleaned their CRM before asking AI to enrich it.

That work looked slow and maybe unambitious.

It didn't show up in any board deck too.

But it created a foundation where AI inputs are trustworthy.

➡ When your field definitions are consistent, AI enrichment produces clean outputs.
➡ When your lifecycle stages reflect how buyers actually move, scoring works.
➡ When your routing logic is documented rather than tribal knowledge, agents can execute it reliably.
➡ When your data model is clear, every downstream decision (attribution, forecasting, personalization) improves.

**Why I picked this**: Rizzo is the founder of the community whose research he's citing, and the patterns he describes ("teams I watch", "deployments we're seeing in our community") are not abstract claims but direct observations from running a 20,000-person operator community. For a B2B SaaS audience, the post also lands a sharper point than most CLG content: AI hype is downstream of operational hygiene, and community-level visibility into who is doing the unglamorous work is what makes you able to spot real signal vs vendor narrative.

## Post 3 — The AI gap is judgment, not tools

**Date**: 2026-05-24 (approx, "4weeks")
**Link**: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mikedrizzo_theres-a-lot-of-pressure-right-now-to-get-activity-7463196960116621312-jy4c?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFZQPK8BcXnoBwVbAgFxMUiN2Lddij6pz3k
**Engagement**: 29 reactions, 9 comments

There's a lot of pressure right now to "get better at AI."

Everyone is talking about
Learning the tools.
Understanding the capabilities.
And figure out how to apply them.

But when we look at how this is playing out inside teams, something different shows up.

The tools are improving quickly.
Access is expanding (slowly).
But outcomes are uneven.
And the gap isn't technical.
It's judgment.

Because AI doesn't decide what matters.
It operates within the context we give it:

→ what data it can access
→ how that data is structured
→ what outcomes we define
→ what decisions we allow it to make

If that context is weak, the output is inconsistent.
If that context is strong, the same tools produce very different results.

That's the shift happening underneath all of this.

The value is moving away from:
"Who knows how to use the tool?"

Toward:
"Who understands the system well enough to guide it?"

The teams getting the most out of AI right now aren't just more technical.

They're the ones who understand:
→ their data
→ their customers
→ their system behavior

And can connect those pieces into decisions.

Remember,
AI doesn't replace thinking.
It makes the quality of our thinking visibly faster.

**Why I picked this**: For a role described as "AI-Native Growth Marketing Specialist", this post is directly on-thesis. Rizzo separates AI capability from AI outcomes, and locates the difference in judgment grounded in customer and system understanding, which is exactly what a community-built knowledge base produces. The post also models how a community leader makes pattern claims responsibly: by anchoring them in observations of teams inside his own community rather than generic predictions.
