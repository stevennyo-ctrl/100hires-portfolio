## Post 1 - Five things she wishes every leader knew about customer trust

**Date**: ~June 14, 2026 (1 week ago)
**Link**: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/christinamgarnett_i-get-dms-almost-daily-with-people-wanting-activity-7470522674075783168-_f_g?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFZQPK8BcXnoBwVbAgFxMUiN2Lddij6pz3k
**Engagement**: 14 reactions, 2 comments, 3 shares

I get DMs almost daily with people wanting to pick my brain. They want tips, insights, and "what I wish I could tell every leader."

So for the sake of my inmail, here are some of them.

1. Stop treating trust as a reward you hand out. It's infrastructure you build.

Most brand leaders think of trust as something customers earn their way into, or something that follows a good experience. That gets the architecture backwards. Trust has to be designed into how your organization operates before a single customer interaction happens. If your systems, incentives, and internal culture aren't built for consistency and care, no amount of good intentions survives contact with a bad and increasingly inhuman process.

Is trust important for customer brand relationships? Well, I only mention it on 135 out of 298 pages of my book (see search screenshot).

2. Friction is a trust withdrawal.

Every time a customer has to repeat themselves, fight a policy, or wait longer than they should, you're taking something from the relationship. Brands track NPS and CSAT but rarely audit the cumulative weight of small frictions. Those small friction moments compound. The Customer Trust Equation has friction as the only subtracted variable for a reason.

3. Your customers are a social body.

They talk to each other. They form norms, share interpretations of your behavior, and collectively decide what your brand means. Sociologists have understood this for over a century. Brand leaders are still largely acting as if communication flows one direction. What happens to one customer becomes a signal to all of them.

4. Anomie is a brand crisis you won't see coming.

When a brand changes, rebrands, restructures, or deprioritizes community, customers experience something close to what Durkheim described as social anomie: the rules they understood no longer apply. That disorientation becomes silence, churn, and disengagement. If you're seeing customers go quiet, ask what changed. They may be mourning something you took from them.

5. Loyalty programs are not loyalty.

They are retention mechanics. Loyalty is relational. It comes from customers who feel seen, whose values align with yours, and who would choose you even without a discount. Brands have spent years building behavioral lock-in and calling it loyalty. When conditions change and the perks go away, so does the relationship, because there was no relationship. Build for connection, not just buying triggers.

**Why I picked this**: Five specific frameworks distilled from her trust research. Each one reframes a common assumption: trust is infrastructure not a reward, friction is a trust withdrawal not just a UX issue, customers form a social body not isolated buyers, anomie causes invisible brand crises, loyalty programs are retention mechanics not actual loyalty. For 100Hires, where trust between platform, recruiter, and candidate is the whole business, all five translate directly to product and growth decisions.

## Post 2 - Belonging is not a vibe, it is a decision

**Date**: 2026-06-21 (1 hour before collection)

✨ Belonging is not a vibe. It is a decision. ✨

We need community now more than ever but we can't just hope we create warm and fuzzy feelings.

We need to choose it and build it.

Here's the TL;DR slide of my HeyOrca talk at the Community-Led Marketing Summit where I discussed the connection between community, advocacy, and sociology.

Can't wait for next year's summit!

[From the TL;DR slide attached to the post:]

Belonging is not a vibe. It is a decision.

Durkheim knew communities without norms collapse. Goffman knew people need a role to inhabit. Putnam knew trust travels through relationships, not channels. Maslow knew you can't skip the foundation.

You can build an advocacy program with a brief and a Notion doc. You cannot build belonging that way. Belonging requires designing the conditions: the norms, the roles, the connections, the safety. All of it has to exist before you ask anyone to show up for you.

Take the Advocacy Systems Diagnostic.

Durkheim: Norms create belonging
Goffman: Roles shape identity
Putnam: Trust travels through relationships
Maslow: Foundation must be real

**Why I picked this**: Christina anchors community-led growth in sociology instead of vibes. She names four foundational thinkers (Durkheim on norms, Goffman on roles, Putnam on relational trust, Maslow on foundation) and uses them to explain why belonging requires designing the conditions before you ask anyone to show up. Her line that you can build an advocacy program with a brief and a Notion doc, but you cannot build belonging that way, is a useful check on any B2B SaaS that's treating community as just another content channel.

## Post 3 - The 2026 trust collapse and what brands can do about it

**Date**: 2026-06-21 (3 hours before collection)
**Link**: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/christinamgarnett_overview-and-key-findings-of-the-2026-digital-activity-7472994140256104448-paz5?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFZQPK8BcXnoBwVbAgFxMUiN2Lddij6pz3k
**Engagement**: 9 reactions, 2 comments, 2 shares

The Reuters Institute for the Study of Journalism released their 2026 Digital News Report this week, and trust is exactly where you would expect it to be, considering misinformation, AI, loss of norms, and {waves hands frantically at everything else}.

Global trust in news has dropped to 37%, the lowest since they started measuring it in 2015. In the US, it's 25%.

A deeper issue is that even though people don't trust these news sources, they are actively leaning towards outlets we all know can be untrustworthy too. Social media and video networks now outrank every traditional news source globally, while trust in news on those same platforms sits at 22%.

Audiences are still scrolling even when they don't trust what they are seeing.

News isn't the only thing seeing a general lost of trust from the public. The person who doesn't believe the news, doesn't want to believe their inboxes or brands' sales messages.

The world that creates a cynic does not create someone cynical of just one thing. If people are losing trust with everything in the world, that inherently makes brand trust more fragile.

What can brands do to show that they are the one thing you can trust when it seems the whole world can't be trusted?

https://lnkd.in/ehnb7ZvE

**Why I picked this**: Hard data on the broader trust collapse that explains why community-led growth matters now. Global trust in news at 37%, US at 25%, social media at 22%. Christina connects the dots: the world that creates a cynic does not create someone cynical of just one thing. So brand cynicism follows news cynicism follows institutional cynicism. The question she ends with, what can a brand do to be the trustworthy one when nothing else is, is the strategic question community-led growth tries to answer.
