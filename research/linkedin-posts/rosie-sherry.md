## Post 1 — Valuing the work you create (Rosieland pricing)

**Date**: 2026-06-14 (approx, "1week")
**Link**: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/rosiesherry_a-year-or-so-ago-i-bumped-up-the-price-tag-activity-7470848932504559616-Xdw7?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFZQPK8BcXnoBwVbAgFxMUiN2Lddij6pz3k
**Engagement**: 12 reactions, 1 comment, 1 share

A year or so ago, I bumped up the price tag on my Rosieland. It's not really a newsletter, though most people see it that way. It's my deep, active research that I share with those who are willing to listen.

I remain adamant that you can't find what I share anywhere else. It's built on true stories of me being deep in doing the community work.

But also, here is the thing. The deeper I go, the more excited I get. The more I see that people aren't seeing. I'm excited to keep going.

And I won't share it publicly, because y'know, people and tech bros. And I simply value my work.

And even if no one subscribes, it's still useful for me. Like today, I can feed my work into Claude and map out a workshop in a couple of hours. Without my written work, that would take me days.

There are many different ways to value the work you create.

I'll probably bump the price up again shortly and start shipping some new things, somehow!

**Why I picked this**: Sherry articulates a counterintuitive monetization stance: build the product for yourself first, value the work intrinsically, then let pricing follow. For B2B SaaS founders thinking about community as a growth channel, this reframes the typical question of "how do I monetize my community" into "how do I value the research and writing the community produces". The casual mention of feeding her own work into Claude to scaffold a workshop is also a small but sharp signal about how operators are using AI on top of their owned content.

## Post 3 — Surprises as invitations to learn (amplifying a community member)

**Date**: 2026-06-07 (approx, "2weeks")
**Link**: https://www.linkedin.com/posts/rosiesherry_if-youre-surprised-by-a-product-behaviour-activity-7467174370591813632-6wto?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAFZQPK8BcXnoBwVbAgFxMUiN2Lddij6pz3k
**Engagement**: 4 reactions, 2 comment

🔥 If you're surprised by a product behaviour, you don't understand the system enough

In TWiQ Oleksandr Romanov said:

"My hot take of the week: If you don't fully understand the thing you want to test and measure - you might get the results that are surprising but yet mysterious. So - first - understand how things work, then try to measure it."

This made me think about framing how we see things. We are often experience surprising software behaviour, but really if we understood the system then we wouldn't be surprised to see things happen.

So when we see surprising behaviour then:
- we need to question what it is we understand
- or what it is we need to spend some time learning
- or what are the gaps and risks that we've missed

Surprises are, therefore, an invitation to learn.

**Why I picked this**: This is the cleanest example of practice-what-you-preach in the set. Sherry doesn't just write about community-led growth, she behaves it: her LinkedIn post amplifies a community member's quote (Oleksandr Romanov) and builds a small piece of thinking on top of it. For B2B SaaS founders, this is the operating pattern most often missed: your community's collective intelligence is your content engine if you bother to listen and quote.
