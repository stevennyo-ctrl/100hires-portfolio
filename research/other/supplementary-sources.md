# Supplementary Sources

The 10 experts in `sources.md` form the core research base. This file 
collects four supplementary pieces that either reinforce, complicate, 
or directly challenge the patterns I found. Two are critiques of CLG, 
one is an active practitioner publication, and one is a 2026 framing 
piece on AI-era moats.

I read these after building the core synthesis. They sharpened the 
"What's Missing" section in `patterns-and-tensions.md` more than any 
single source from the core 10.

---

## 1. "Community-Led Growth Is Mostly a Distraction" (Momentum Nexus)

**URL**: https://www.momentumnexus.com/blog/community-led-growth-b2b-saas

**Format**: Long-form blog post, ~3,000 words, published Q2 2026.

**Core argument**: For most B2B SaaS companies at $50K to $150K MRR, CLG 
is a distraction from channels that will actually move the number. The 
post cites CMX 2025 data showing 76% of companies running community 
programs cannot quantify financial impact. The author argues the celebrated 
CLG success stories (Slack, Figma, Notion) had structural advantages: 
built-in product network effects, ICP that publicly shares work, 
horizontal product flexibility. Most B2B SaaS companies do not have these 
preconditions and therefore copy tactics from contexts where they cannot 
succeed.

**Why this matters for the synthesis**: This is the single sharpest 
articulation of the "scale-stage bias" critique I flagged in 
`patterns-and-tensions.md`. The 76% measurement failure stat is the 
operator number that makes the critique defensible to a CFO.

**Where I partially disagree**: The author treats CLG as binary 
(distraction vs. growth driver). My read from the 10 expert collection 
is that CLG has a stage threshold. Below product-market fit it really is 
a distraction. After PMF but pre-scale, it can be the most efficient 
retention lever available. The post collapses these stages.

**Recommended for**: Anyone arguing the business case for or against a 
community program. The skepticism here is operator-grade, not contrarian 
for sport.

## 2. "A Realistic Guide to Community-Led Growth" (Rewardful)

**URL**: https://www.rewardful.com/articles/community-led-growth

**Format**: Mid-length guide, ~2,000 words.

**Core argument**: Most B2B SaaS companies "are getting CLG completely 
wrong, due to incomplete and inaccurate advice out there." Author argues 
social followers and newsletter subscribers are NOT a community. A 
community requires member-to-member interaction, not just brand-to-member 
broadcast. The piece is anti-hype and practical: build by joining other 
people's communities first to understand what makes them work.

**Why this matters for the synthesis**: Reinforces the "audience vs. 
community conflation" critique I made in `patterns-and-tensions.md` 
about Lloyed Lobo counting newsletter subscribers as community members. 
This Rewardful piece explicitly draws the same line.

**Practical takeaway used in `applied-case-study.md`**: The "spend 
months joining other people's communities before building one" advice 
maps directly onto Play 2 (founder-as-model-member). The piece 
strengthens the case for that play.

**Recommended for**: Anyone about to launch a community without first 
participating in one.

## 3. Rosieland by Rosie Sherry (Active practitioner newsletter)

**URL**: https://rosie.land/ and https://rosieland.substack.com/

**Format**: Weekly newsletter (paid + free tiers), plus knowledge base.

**Why include this beyond the core sources**: Rosie Sherry is in my 
core 10 (Tier A). But her newsletter itself is the more useful artifact 
than any single LinkedIn post. Rosieland is a working example of 
"community for community builders," which is one of the few CLG verticals 
where the practitioner publishes their pricing, member counts, and 
workflow experiments openly. The newsletter is what most community 
operators read to stay current.

**What it adds**: Specific tactical posts on AI workflows for community 
managers, member spotlight mechanics, and the experiment-in-public 
methodology Rosie uses. These are the kind of operator-level details that 
do not survive into LinkedIn posts.

**Where to start**: The free archive on rosie.land covers most of the 
foundational frameworks. The paid tier covers the more experimental 
content.

**Why I am citing this separately**: Including the newsletter as a 
supplementary source (rather than additional LinkedIn posts) acknowledges 
that not all expert output lives on LinkedIn or YouTube. A research 
methodology that ignores newsletters misses where many operators do 
their highest-signal writing.

## 4. "20 Micro-SaaS Ideas for 2026 (That AI Won't Kill)" (StartuPage)

**URL**: https://startupa.ge/blog/micro-saas-ideas-2026

**Format**: Long-form ideation guide, ~5,000 words.

**Core argument**: With 35% of point-product SaaS predicted to be replaced 
by AI agents by 2030 (cites Deloitte 2025), the only durable advantages 
left for solo founders and small teams are data moats, community moats, 
and network effects. The piece is not specifically about CLG, but its 
"community moat" framing directly answers the AI-native gap I flagged in 
`patterns-and-tensions.md`.

**Why this matters for the synthesis**: None of the core 10 experts 
walked through what community design looks like when AI agents become 
participants, members, or competitors to community itself. This piece 
addresses the post-2026 framing more directly. It also provides the 
"why now" argument for SMB B2B SaaS in this market: AI is hollowing out 
the content moat that previously distinguished competitors, so the 
community moat becomes structurally more valuable.

**Where to be skeptical**: The piece is bullish on micro-SaaS in general 
and may overstate AI replacement timelines for B2B SaaS specifically. 
The 35% replacement-by-2030 figure is a Deloitte projection, not 
realized data.

**Recommended for**: Framing why CLG matters MORE in 2026 than in 2020, 
not less.
