# 10 Community Operators, Read Closely

### What they agree on, where they disagree, and what their playbooks miss

I collected 30 LinkedIn posts and 10 YouTube transcripts from 10 people 
who build communities for a living. This file is what I learned from 
reading their work side by side.

It assumes you have already skimmed [`sources.md`](sources.md). If you 
have not, start there.

---

**TL;DR**
- They all agree community is the strongest moat in an AI-saturated market.
- They disagree on org placement, platform choice, measurement standards, 
  and volunteer reliance.
- They mostly avoid: failure stories, early-stage realities, and what 
  community looks like when AI does 80% of customer interactions.

---

## 3 Things They Actually Agree On

### 1. Community is a moat, not a channel.

Most growth tactics decay as competitors copy them. Community does not, 
because the asset is the relationships between members, not the brand 
running it.

- **Lloyed Lobo**: "If you build a community, you won't become a commodity. 
  Yesterday's innovation always becomes today's option and tomorrow's commodity."
- **David Spinks** (in *Business of Belonging*): community produces an 
  asset competitors cannot replicate at any spend level.
- **Erica Kuhl** (Salesforce to Gainsight): the Trailblazer community 
  became the single biggest reason customers stayed on Salesforce, more 
  than the product itself.

**Implication for B2B SaaS**: Community ROI is back-loaded. You can copy 
a competitor's ad copy in a day. You cannot copy 3 years of accumulated 
trust between members.

---

### 2. Audience first, product community second.

A consistent pattern in their stories: they built an audience around a 
topic BEFORE building a community around their product.

- **Lloyed Lobo** built the Traction conference (100K+ subscribers around 
  startup growth) for years before Boast.AI's product community.
- **Mike Rizzo** started MarketingOps as a free Slack channel in 2017. 
  MarketingOps.com (the paid product) came in 2019, two years later.
- **HubSpot** (cited by Lloyed) ran "inbound marketing" content for years 
  before the HubSpot product community existed.

The recurring pattern is that **community often emerges from an audience, 
not the other way around**. The reverse, where you build a product then 
bolt a community on top, has almost no success stories in this collection.

**Implication for B2B SaaS**: If you only have a product community (users 
of your tool), you have a support channel, not a community. The audience 
community (people interested in the broader topic your product serves) is 
what drives growth.

---

### 3. Measurement is about translation, not invention.

Community leaders who survive long-term measure community in the language 
the rest of the business already uses.

- **Erica Kuhl**: "I had to figure out what each organization needs, 
  speak their language. Sales wants revenue. Customer success wants 
  retention. I never showed them page views."
- **Brian Oblinger** (CMX Summit 2022): walked through how Alteryx, 
  data IQ, and Bunnings tied community to deflection cost, customer 
  lifetime value, and revenue, not to engagement metrics.
- **Mike Rizzo**: "MarketingOps is a product function. It has a roadmap, 
  stakeholders, and an ROI like any other product."

**Implication for B2B SaaS**: If your community dashboard only shows 
MAU and post counts, you have no defense against the next budget cut. 
Translate every number into pipeline, retention, or cost saved.

## Who I Trust Most, For What

Reading these 10 operators side by side, they are not equally useful. 
If I had to pick one expert per problem:

- **Erica Kuhl** for measurement and org politics. She built the most 
  ROI-defensible community in B2B SaaS history.
- **Lloyed Lobo** for early-stage scrappy execution. He was a team of 
  two doing pizza nights long before Traction had 100K subscribers. 
  Closer to most startups' reality.
- **Brian Oblinger** for the hard ROI conversation. His CMX Summit 2022 
  talk is the single best "how to defend community budget to a CFO" 
  content I found.
- **Mike Rizzo** for the community-as-product mental model. Most 
  underrated framing in the collection.

For the rest: useful contributors, but I would not stake a 100Hires 
strategy on any one of them alone.

## 4 Things They Disagree On (The Interesting Part)

When experts disagree, the disagreement itself maps a decision you have 
to make.

### Tension 1: Where the community team sits in the org

- **Erica Kuhl**: started in Marketing at Salesforce, hated it, moved 
  to Product. The community exploded. Argues community should be near 
  the product team or directly under C-suite, not under Marketing's 
  lead-gen pressure.
- **Mike Rizzo**: argues community IS a product function, fully separate 
  from Marketing. The MarketingOps community has its own roadmap, 
  pricing, stakeholders.
- **Joel Primack**: works on Salesforce community within a 
  Marketing-adjacent team. Defends Marketing alignment for top-of-funnel 
  goals.

**My read**: At early-stage SaaS where there is no separate product team, 
community sits under whoever has budget and patience. The real danger 
is lead-gen pressure short-circuiting community ROI before it matures.

---

### Tension 2: Slack/Discord vs owned platform

- **Mike Rizzo**: built MarketingOps on Slack since 2017. Says the 
  friction-free entry beat any owned platform's analytics depth.
- **Erica Kuhl**: built Trailblazer on Salesforce's owned platform 
  specifically to control data, taxonomy, and product feedback flow.
- **David Spinks**: Notion's biggest community lives on a subreddit 
  Notion did not create. Advises: "embrace it, don't fight it."

**My read**: For 100Hires-scale companies, start on Slack and migrate 
later. Owned platforms only pay off once you need product-feedback-to-CRM 
tie-ins or formal data ownership. Neither is urgent in the first 12 months.

---

### Tension 3: Measuring "in the wild" vs hard ROI

- **Joel Primack** (interviewing Joe Huber from Sprout Social): "We look 
  for value in the wild. Spotting a community member share value with 
  another member without us touching it, that's a win, even if we can't 
  measure it."
- **Brian Oblinger** (CMX Summit 2022): pushes back hard. "You walk into 
  a CEO's office talking about unique visitors, you're in big trouble. 
  Measurement programs need to produce dollars, not screenshots."
- **Erica Kuhl**: bridges both. "You need both health metrics and 
  business north-star metrics. You don't get business metrics overnight, 
  but you need the eye on them."

**My read**: "Value in the wild" is genuine but cannot be your only 
metric, especially in a downturn. Use it as the *engagement* layer, then 
pair with hard ROI for *business* layer. Brian's framework (deflection 
cost + customer lifetime value + revenue) is the safer default.

---

### Tension 4: Volunteer leaders vs paid staff

- **Lloyed Lobo**: Traction ran on 40 to 60 volunteers per conference. 
  He emphasizes "give volunteers the spotlight", let them MC, let them lead.
- **Erica Kuhl**: Salesforce MVP program was structured, NDA-bound, with 
  Salesforce employees deeply involved. Less volunteer-driven, more curated.
- **David Spinks**: CMX Connect program scaled to 60 chapters using a 
  vetted volunteer model. High bar to entry, then autonomy.

**My read**: Volunteer-led scales fast and cheap, but only if your 
audience already trusts the brand. For an unknown early-stage brand, 
you need 1 to 2 paid community managers to build the playbook first, then 
transition to volunteer leaders for scale.

## What's Missing In Their Playbooks

This is what surprised me. These 10 operators are excellent, but their 
content has consistent blind spots.

**1. They don't talk about failure modes.**  
Every transcript is a success story. None of them walked through "here is 
a community I tried to build that died at 200 members and why." For an 
early-stage SaaS deciding whether to invest 6 to 12 months in community, 
the failure case studies would be more useful than the success ones.

**2. They mostly talk about scale-stage businesses.**  
Trailblazer (Salesforce, public company). Boast.AI ($10M ARR at the time 
of community success). MarketingOps (already had product-market fit). 
Almost nothing in the 10 transcripts addresses: "What if I have 50 
customers and one community manager?"

**3. AI-native operating model is barely addressed.**  
Most experts mention AI in passing, as a threat to community ("AI can't 
replicate trust") or as a workflow tool ("we use AI to summarize threads"). 
None walked through what community design looks like when AI handles 80% 
of customer interactions, or when AI agents become community members 
themselves. This is a gap, not a timing excuse. Lloyed Lobo, Erica Kuhl, 
and Mike Rizzo all post weekly in 2024 to 2025. They have bandwidth, they 
just have not prioritized this question.

**4. They conflate audience and community when convenient.**  
Lloyed counts 100K Traction subscribers as "community." But subscribers 
are an audience, not a community. Members don't talk to each other 
through a subscription. This conflation makes ROI claims fuzzier than 
they need to be.

## If I Were Running This Tomorrow, I Would Test

Three plays I would run at an early-stage AI-native company. Detailed 
in [`applied-to-100hires.md`](applied-to-100hires.md). Summarized here.

**1. The "Trojan content" hypothesis.**  
Build the audience around a topic adjacent to the product (e.g., 
AI-native recruiting workflows), not the product itself. Audience first, 
community second, product third. Measure: audience growth, content 
shares, inbound interest in tooling.

**2. The "model member" play (from Noele Flowers).**  
Have one founder or team member visibly participate in 3 to 5 external 
recruiting communities weekly for 90 days. Not as a brand, as a person. 
Measure: inbound DMs, demo requests, brand mentions.

**3. The "deflection" measurement (from Brian Oblinger).**  
Track every customer question answered in community vs raised in support 
tickets. Even at small scale, this is the most defensible community ROI 
metric, and the one most easily reported to a CFO.

## What This Means for 100Hires

The detailed application is in [`applied-to-100hires.md`](applied-to-100hires.md).

If there is one pattern across all ten operators, it is this: the 
strongest communities were not built as marketing campaigns. They were 
built as products, with stakeholders, roadmaps, and 12 to 18 month 
horizons. Growth showed up at the end, not the beginning.

The companies that treated community as a quarterly lead-gen channel 
mostly don't appear in this collection. They didn't survive long enough 
to write about it.
